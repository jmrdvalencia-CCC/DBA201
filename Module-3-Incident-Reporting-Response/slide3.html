Digital Forensics
Awareness

When a breach happens, evidence is everything. As an accountant, you won't conduct the investigation — but you can make or break it by how you handle evidence in the first critical minutes.

📄 Based on ISO/IEC 27037 — Digital Evidence Identification, Collection & Preservation
Part 2.1 · Evidence Types
Volatile vs Non-Volatile Evidence
Volatile evidence disappears when a device is powered off or restarted. This is why you should NEVER shut down your computer during an incident unless IT instructs you to.
⚡ Volatile Evidence (Disappears on Power-Off)
🧠
RAM contents — active malware, decryption keys, open documents currently in memory
🌐
Active network connections — which servers the attacker is communicating with right now
⚙️
Running processes — malicious programs currently executing on your machine
📋
Clipboard contents — data the attacker may have copied (passwords, account numbers)
🔑
Logged-in sessions — active authentication tokens that prove who accessed what
💾 Non-Volatile Evidence (Survives Power-Off)
📁
Files on disk — documents, spreadsheets, databases stored on hard drive or SSD
📧
Email archives — sent/received messages, attachments, headers with routing info
📊
System logs — Windows Event Logs, application logs, security audit trails
🌐
Browser history — URLs visited, cached pages, saved passwords, cookies
📱
Cloud audit logs — Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, QuickBooks sign-in records
⚡ Volatile evidence is often the most valuable for investigators — it shows what the attacker was doing at that exact moment. Shutting down your computer destroys it permanently.
Part 2.2 · Evidence You'll Encounter
6 Types of Evidence Accountants Handle
You interact with these daily. During an incident, each becomes a forensic artifact.
📧
Phishing Emails
Full email headers contain sender IP, routing path, and spoofing indicators. Save as .eml — don't just screenshot.
Save as .eml attachment
📊
Sign-In Logs
Microsoft 365 / Google Workspace logs show IP addresses, locations, device types, and timestamps of every login.
Screenshot + export CSV
📁
File Version History
OneDrive, SharePoint, and Google Drive track every edit. Version history shows who changed what and when.
Do NOT edit the file
💬
Chat Messages
Teams/Slack messages can be deleted by attackers. Screenshot suspicious conversations immediately — include timestamps.
Screenshot with timestamps
🔔
Error Messages & Alerts
Exact wording of error messages, security alerts, or unusual pop-ups. These help IT identify the specific attack vector.
Copy exact text + screenshot
📋
Your Own Timeline Notes
A written record of what you observed, when, and what actions you took. This becomes a legal document in investigations.
Write immediately — memory fades
Part 2.3 · Chain of Custody
The Chain of Custody — Why It Matters
For evidence to be admissible in legal proceedings or NPC investigations, there must be an unbroken record of who handled it and when. Breaking the chain = evidence thrown out.
1
You discover the evidence
Note the exact date, time, and what you observed. Do NOT touch, modify, or move the evidence.
2
You document and report
Screenshot/save evidence. Record your name, the time, and exactly what you did. Report to IT Security.
3
IT Security takes custody
IT creates forensic copies (disk images) using write-blockers. Original evidence is sealed and logged.
4
Analysis on forensic copies only
Investigators work on copies — never the original. Every action is logged with timestamps and analyst names.
5
Evidence stored securely
Originals stored in tamper-evident bags or encrypted vaults. Access log maintained for legal admissibility.
Part 2.4 · Critical Actions
What Destroys vs Preserves Evidence
Your actions in the first 5 minutes determine whether investigators can trace the attacker — or not.
🚫 Actions That DESTROY Evidence
❌ Shutting down or restarting the computer — kills volatile memory
❌ Deleting suspicious emails — removes headers, routing data, attachments
❌ Clearing browser history — erases URLs, cookies, cached credentials
❌ Running antivirus scans yourself — may quarantine evidence before IT can image it
❌ Editing or opening suspicious files — changes metadata (last accessed timestamp)
❌ Forwarding phishing emails normally — strips original headers
❌ Disconnecting from network without instruction — may alert the attacker
✅ Actions That PRESERVE Evidence
✅ Leave the computer ON and untouched — volatile evidence stays intact
✅ Save suspicious emails as .eml files — preserves full headers
✅ Screenshot everything with timestamps — visual proof of what you saw
✅ Write down exact error messages — word-for-word, including codes
✅ Note the exact time you noticed the anomaly — incident timeline starts here
✅ Lock your screen (Win+L) — prevents further tampering without powering off
✅ Call IT Security verbally — don't use the potentially compromised device to report
🔬 In the 2023 Verizon DBIR, 37% of breach investigations were hampered by evidence destruction — most of it accidental, by well-meaning employees trying to "fix" the problem themselves.
