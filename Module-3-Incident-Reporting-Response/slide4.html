Incident Severity
Classification & Triage

Not all incidents are equal. A phishing email caught by a spam filter is not the same as an active data exfiltration. Understanding severity levels helps you communicate urgency accurately — and ensures the right people respond at the right speed.

Part 3.1 · Severity Levels
The 4 Severity Levels
Based on NIST and ISO 27035. Most organizations use a P1–P4 scale. Your report should include the severity you believe applies.
P1
Critical — Active Breach
Confirmed data exfiltration, ransomware spreading, or active attacker inside the network. Client data actively being stolen or destroyed.
⏱ Respond: 15 min
Resolve: 4 hours
CISO + DPO + Legal + Management
P2
High — Likely Breach
Strong indicators of compromise — unauthorized access confirmed, but data exposure not yet verified. Compromised credentials with access to sensitive systems.
⏱ Respond: 1 hour
Resolve: 24 hours
IT Security + DPO + Department Head
P3
Medium — Suspicious Activity
Anomalies detected but no confirmed compromise — unusual login patterns, failed MFA attempts, suspicious emails received but not clicked.
⏱ Respond: 4 hours
Resolve: 72 hours
IT Security + Supervisor
P4
Low — Near-Miss / Informational
Blocked phishing email, failed login from unknown IP, policy violation caught early. No data exposure, but worth documenting for trend analysis.
⏱ Respond: 24 hours
Resolve: 1 week
IT Helpdesk + Logged for review
Part 3.2 · Triage Questions
The 5-Question Triage — Assess Severity Fast
When you detect something suspicious, run through these 5 questions mentally. More "Yes" answers = higher severity.
1
Is sensitive data (client PII, financials, TINs) potentially exposed?
Yes → Escalate to P1/P2No → Continue assessment
2
Is the threat still active right now? (ongoing unauthorized access, spreading malware)
Yes → P1 CriticalNo → P2 or P3
3
How many people/clients are potentially affected?
Many (10+) → Higher priorityJust me → Lower priority
4
Can the affected system be isolated without stopping business operations?
Yes → Containment easierNo → Needs management decision
5
Does this trigger a legal notification requirement? (NPC 72-hour rule)
Yes → DPO must be notified NOWNo → Standard response
Part 3.3 · Escalation Matrix
Who Gets Called and When
Different severity levels trigger different escalation paths. Know who to contact before an incident happens.
🖥️
IT Helpdesk
All incidents — your first point of contact for any security concern
All Levels
🛡️
IT Security Team
P1–P3 incidents requiring investigation, containment, or forensic analysis
P1–P3
⚖️
Data Protection Officer
Any incident involving personal data — determines NPC notification obligation
P1–P2
👔
Senior Management / CISO
P1 critical incidents — business impact decisions, client notification, legal counsel
P1 Only
⚠️ You don't need to determine the exact severity. Your job is to report what you observed — the incident response team will classify it. But understanding severity helps you communicate urgency: "I think this might be a P1" gets faster attention than "something seems weird."
Part 3.4 · Accounting Examples
Severity in Accounting Context
Real scenarios mapped to severity levels — so you can calibrate your response.
P1 Critical
Ransomware encrypts the firm's QuickBooks database and shared drives. Client financial records inaccessible. Ransom note displayed.
Call IT Security + Management NOW
P1 Critical
Attacker uses compromised email to send BEC payment redirect to 12 clients. ₱3.2M in fraudulent transfers initiated.
Call IT + DPO + Legal NOW
P2 High
Microsoft 365 sign-in log shows successful login from Vietnam at 3 AM. Your account has access to 50 client tax returns.
Report to IT within 1 hour
P3 Medium
You receive 5 MFA push notifications you didn't trigger over 10 minutes. No successful login detected yet.
Report to IT within 4 hours
P3 Medium
A colleague's email sends you a link to "review this invoice" — but the colleague says they didn't send it.
Report + don't click
P4 Low
Spam filter catches a phishing email impersonating BIR. No one clicked it. Filter worked correctly.
Log as near-miss
