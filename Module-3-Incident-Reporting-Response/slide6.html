📝
Writing Effective
Incident Reports

A well-written incident report accelerates investigation, satisfies legal requirements, and protects your firm. A poorly written one wastes hours and may render evidence inadmissible. Here's how to get it right.

Part 5.1 · The Framework
The 5W1H Incident Reporting Framework
Every incident report must answer these 6 questions. If any is missing, the report is incomplete.
WHAT
What happened?
Exact description of the anomaly. "Received MFA push notification I didn't trigger" — not "something weird happened."
WHEN
When did you notice it?
Exact date and time: "April 15, 2025, 2:35 PM PHT." Include timezone. The NPC 72-hour clock starts from discovery.
WHERE
Which system/account?
"Microsoft 365 — Outlook and OneDrive." Be specific: platform, application, account name, device type.
WHO
Who is affected / who reported?
Your name, role, and which clients/employees may be impacted. "15 client tax returns in the compromised OneDrive folder."
WHY
Why do you think it's suspicious?
"Sign-in log shows login from Lagos, Nigeria at 2:30 PM — I was in Calamba." Explain what made you report it.
HOW
What actions did you take?
"Changed password immediately. Did NOT access files. Saved screenshot of sign-in log. Reported via Teams to IT Security."
💡 Think of 5W1H as a checklist before you submit. If you can't answer one of these, note it as "Unknown" — that's still useful information for investigators.
Part 5.2 · Sample Report
A Model Incident Report
This is what a well-structured first-responder report looks like. Use this as your template.
🔒 Incident Report — First Responder
Severity: P2 — High
📅 Date & Time
April 15, 2025, 2:35 PM PHT
👤 Reporter
Maria Santos, Staff Accountant, Tax Department
📝 Description
Received unsolicited MFA push notification at 2:32 PM. Checked Microsoft 365 sign-in logs — found successful login from Lagos, Nigeria (IP: 102.xxx.xxx.xx) at 2:30 PM. I was physically in Calamba, Laguna at the time. I did NOT approve the MFA prompt.
💻 Systems
Microsoft 365 — Outlook, OneDrive, SharePoint
📂 Data at Risk
15 client Q1 2025 tax returns, payroll files for 3 clients, email correspondence with clients containing TINs and bank details
✅ Actions Taken
1) Changed M365 password from personal phone
2) Did NOT access OneDrive or open any files
3) Screenshotted sign-in log with foreign IP
4) Saved suspicious MFA notification
5) Reported to IT Security via phone call at 2:38 PM
🔒 Evidence
Screenshot of sign-in log (saved to desktop as incident-2025-04-15.png). MFA notification still visible in authenticator app history.
Part 5.3 · Good vs Bad
Vague vs Precise Reporting
The difference between a report that helps investigators and one that wastes their time.
❌ Vague Report (Wastes Time)
What: "Something weird happened with my email"
When: "Earlier today, I think"
Where: "My computer"
Who: "Me"
Actions: "I changed my password and deleted the email"
Evidence: "I don't have any, I deleted it"
✅ Precise Report (Accelerates Response)
What: "Unsolicited MFA prompt + foreign sign-in on M365"
When: "April 15, 2025, 2:35 PM PHT"
Where: "Microsoft 365 — Outlook, OneDrive, SharePoint"
Who: "Maria Santos, Staff Accountant — 15 clients at risk"
Actions: "Changed password from separate device. Did NOT access files. Reported to IT at 2:38 PM."
Evidence: "Screenshot of sign-in log saved. Phishing email saved as .eml."
📋 The vague report forces IT to spend 30+ minutes asking follow-up questions. The precise report lets them start investigating immediately. In a P1 incident, those 30 minutes could mean thousands more records stolen.
